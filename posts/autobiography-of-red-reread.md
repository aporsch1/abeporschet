I first read Anne Carson's *Autobiography of Red* in college, rushed through it in a few days, and remembered mainly the strangeness: a novel-in-verse about a red-winged boy who photographs volcanoes. Rereading it now, before grad school, I'm struck by how much I missed.

## What I Noticed This Time

The compression. Carson packs entire emotional arcs into single stanzas. When Geryon watches Herakles leave:

> *He felt his wings
> close around him like a shoebox lid.
> He felt the minutes of his life moving
> on him like ants.*

That "shoebox lid"—so specific, so physical. And the ants: not swarming, just *moving*. The restraint is what makes it devastating.

## The Structure

The book begins with essays on Stesichoros, the Greek poet whose fragmentary work Carson uses as scaffolding. First time through, I skimmed these. Now they feel essential—not just scholarly apparatus but a meditation on how we inherit and transform stories.

Carson's version makes Geryon the protagonist, not Herakles. The monster becomes the point of view. What changes when we tell the myth from the perspective of the creature being killed?

## On Translation and Distance

There's a moment where Carson translates a fragment: *"But Herakles shot him down.*" Then adds: "The verb here, *kataballo*, can mean to strike down, to kill, or to put to sleep."

This attention to what's lost in translation, what multiple meanings collapse into single English words—it reminds me of statistical inference, actually. How we take rich, complex reality and compress it into parameters, estimates, p-values. What gets preserved? What gets lost?

## Why It Matters Now

Starting grad school soon means signing up for years of technical work: proofs, simulations, careful argumentation. Carson's book reminds me that precision and beauty aren't opposed. You can be rigorous about metaphor, careful about strangeness.

Geryon's photographs of volcanoes are described as "exercises in passion." Maybe that's what I'm hoping for: work that's technically sound but also reaches toward something larger, stranger, more human than the methods alone would suggest.
