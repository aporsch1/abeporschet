## Reading Bleak House
I'm reading *Bleak House*! This is my first Charles Dickens book since I read *A Tale of Two Cities* in tenth grade. I loved it—it was
one of the first books to ever make me cry, and it has one of my favorite characters in 19th century literature, Sydney Carton. Though I probably
wouldn't consider it one of my all-time favorite books, I remember nearly every major character with startling (for me) lucidity. Brutal Madame
Defarge; sparkling Lucy Manette; even-tempered, and overall 'good guy' Charles Darnay; noble, somewhat self-loathing, but eminently lovable
Sydney Carton. It's a testament to Dickens and his crafting of characters. While I'm only about one sixth of the way through *Bleak House*, I 
find the same eye for detail and for character. 

One fun thing about *Bleak House* is that it alternates every chapter or so between limited third-person narration and first-person narration
from the absolutely *lovely* Esther Summerson. There is a lot to say about Esther, and she is a complicated character (and exists in a complicated
political space), but her niceness, devotion to others, and consummate professionalism can't be argued. She adds a lot of flavor while recounting
stories, and she thinks so positively of everyone that it in effect makes her (what I expect will be) quite an unreliable narrator, particularly
because *Bleak House* is a book about some of society's nastiest tendencies.

But, in any case, without saying much more about *Bleak House*, since I expect to write a full piece once I'm done, I wanted to leave a quote
from the seventh chapter, or so, from when Esther looks out the window of the house belonging to her benefactor John Jarndyce, named...Bleak
House. She arrived late the night prior and didn't get to see the surrounding landscape. When she rises, she moves over to the window and looks
out.

> “As the prospect gradually revealed itself, and disclosed the scene over which the wind had wandered in the dark, like my memory over my life, 
> I had a pleasure in discovering the unknown objects that had been around me in my sleep.”


I was so struck by how wind roving through the dark landscape is compared to the way our memory walks about our lives, and perhaps this wind,
our memory, plays a role in clearing the fog incurred by our lack of immediate comprehension, allowing us, retrospectively, to "discover the unknown
objects that ha[ve] been around [us] in [our] sleep". It's also a reminder of the subjectivity of memory, and how it's driven by whims and drives
which we can only begin to understand. That's why it's so important (and interesting) to examine our epistemic limitations through alternate
modes of understanding things, whether they be narrative explorations (like *Bleak House* or perhaps more fittingly something like *The Sound and
the Fury*—reread soon, by the way) or more structured scientific analysis, using things like statistics. He really writes, on the level of the 
sentence, at a quality nearly nobody can match. Gorgeous.
