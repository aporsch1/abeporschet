##Background

*Bleak House* is a fascinating novel. It feels meandering and plotless while also being a doorstopper around 1000 pages. It simultaneously
seems very progressive and conservative. It's so frequently about abject suffering, repression, poverty, the way systems grow and grow
and obliterate the very things they are meant to protect, but is consistently one of the funniest novels I've read this year (along with *White
Noise* by Don Delillo and *Baron Wenckheim's Homecoming* by Krasznahorkai). 

##Flaws
Before I'm too overwhelming with my praise (all of which is deserved) I feel the need to frontload my main criticism with the novel, which is
that Esther Summerson as a character, and her voice as a narrator can feel overdetermined and forced in order to serve a point Dickens is trying
to make as he feuds with the Brönte sisters (particularly Charlotte and her profound creation, Jane Eyre). Esther is written to be the anti-Jane,
an entirely self-denying figure who exemplifies the role of a 'little woman' who concerns herself most with the needs of others over her own,
which is, after all, a Christian ideal, and not an entirely bad thing, but it leads to uncomfortable places, like her engagement to her guardian,
Mr. Jarndyce. I think Dickens makes these decisions with the intent to display her virtue, but really tells the reader more about him than his 
character. All this said, though, I feel that Esther's character is mostly an accomplishment, with her voice being incredibly enjoyable to spend
time with, and interesting in its unreliability (and Dickens is impressive—and maybe ahead of his time?—with the way he exposes her unreliability
in order to show the reader more about her character).

##The Characters
Now that my hedging is out of the way, I have to say that my favorite part of *Bleak House* is the characters. So many of them are loathsome,
but in such entertaining, brilliant ways. The unbearably dull, stuffy Leicester Dedlock; sinister Mr. Tulkinghorn; the ridiculous philanthropist
Mrs. Jellyby and her cartoonish, woeful family; the model of deportment himself, Mr. Turveydrop; maybe my favorite side-character, Inspector Bucket,
one of the OGs of detective fiction who takes over the novel for about 150 pages; Jo, one of Dickens' great, loveable orphans with a sad end (
who Bucket adorably calls "toughey"); etc. (I could name so many more...the Smallweeds, the orphans, Mrs. Dedlock, Guppy...)

The size of the cast of characters and the vididness of each one is *nonpareil*, up there with some of my favorites like *Infinite Jest,* *J R,*
and even great novels I don't like as much, like *War and Peace,* etc. It has this amazing cast, all wandering around foggy London (which, due
to the number of mentions, you could almost mention the fog as a character of its own), and everyone is, in due time, sucked into the vortex of
*Jarndyce v Jarndyce* a generations old lawsuit over an inheritance. The reach (and eventual outcome) of the case is so perverse and comic, it's
impossible not to see the influence of Dickens in Kafka[^1] and Gaddis, in books like *The Trial* and *J R* (which I've read) and, from what I've heard,
in books like *The Recognitions* and *Amerika.* Each of these characters is written like a sketch from an expert artist. He only needs one or two
sentences to tell nearly everything about a character. The stage is always set perfectly, and then, as details accrue and the picture is fille in,
the characters and situations only get richer and more interesting. 

## Other Assorted Positives
His writing is an example of some of the most sumptuous I've read from the 19th century, while his third person sections are also, unlike many of his contemporaries, or
even unlike himself in some of his other novels, frequently totally taken with the ridiculous and inscrutable nature of large social systems, and less concerned
with more stable, grounded human relationships (though this is balanced out with Esther to make an interesting
and very balanced fusion). For this reason, *Bleak House* seems to peer into the late 20th and early 21st centuries, not unlike the way *Don Quixote*
sees the metafictional and polyphonic aspects of fiction generations later.

Writing Esther or writing the systems aspect of the novel about the court case would both be great achievements, and among the great stories of
the 19th century if published separately, but I think that as a total the strengths and pleasures totally outweigh the flaws of the novel. I'm running
out of things to say without just spewing superlatives, but I really want to highlight the humor of the book. There is loads of wordplay, riffing
on Shakespeare and little details of social and historical interest, some hilarious physical comedy (I loved the sections with the elderly Smallweeds),
comedies of manners, and scathing sarcasm. Dickens finds so many ways to laugh and he's equally successful at all of them.

##TLDR
A massive and beautiful novel that everyone should read, despite some of the dated takes on sexual politics, and the role of women.
