I read *Oblivion* last week over the course of a day or two of sitting in airports, flying on planes, and waiting—in other words, I spent time
with the book under a period of duress, in places more-or-less designed to amplify my loneliness and feelings of emptiness. If you exclude the
distractions that are entailed by time spent in places like airports, you might find that they are the perfect setting to read the late work of
David Foster Wallace, who I think is one of the greatest writers of loneliness ever, and for sure of the late 20th, early 21st century. I love
*Infinite Jest* and I really love his short story "Little Expressionless Animals" (to name writing outside of *Oblivion*).

Reading it, I felt totally pulverized and overcome with emotion. I'm not sure when, or if, I'll write more about the book beyond this Goodreads-style
mini-review that I jotted down shortly after reading.

Nobody writes better about loneliness.

He matches the fractured, digressive, haunted nature of thought, particularly in the age of television (and even internet). Perhaps, 
contra-Joyce, we don't 'forge' our consciousnesses; they are forged for us by the eyes of our fathers as they open the door coming home
from work; by the desert of boredom at work, the resultant, violent daydreams; by the way our minds fill the wire mesh that secures the windows
in 1960s classrooms with vivid and horrifying visions set as comic strips; by the cathartic imagining of the thoughts of a classmate dead by
suicide; by overheard conversations on airplanes.

As the narrator says in The Soul is Not a Smithy, we can tell we are beginning to dream by the unreality at the fringes of our consciousness,
but we fall asleep and dream regardless. What then, when the fringes of consciousness are filled with unreality in our waking moments? We live
"the dream of waking / with alternate endings" regardless (as Ben Lerner puts it in Mean Free Path).
